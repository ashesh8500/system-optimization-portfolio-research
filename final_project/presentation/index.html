02
Thesis

The best recipe changes with the kitchen.

Investing isn't one decision; it's a stack. Where can I shop? What do I put on the list? How much of each? How often do I redo the meal? The textbook answer ("solve for weights") only handles step three. The other steps decide the meal.

We measured every step out of sample. When the shopping list is short, picking the right items matters most. When it's long, ranking them ranks too. When it's medium, calming things down beats chasing winners.

Small kitchen · Top-100

Pick the recent winners. Cook them equally.

21d momentum · equal weight · Sharpe $1.474$ · ret. $20.4\%$ · turnover $\approx 0$

When the menu is already short, the picking does the work. Splitting the plate evenly avoids over-fitting and pays nothing in trading costs.

Medium kitchen · Top-250

Pick the calm ones. Cook them equally.

63d low-vol · equal weight · Sharpe $0.859$ · ret. $11.2\%$ · DD $-10.5\%$

More options also means more noise. Filtering for stability beats chasing trends. A wider menu isn't automatically a better one.

Big kitchen · Top-500

Rank by clean trend. Re-rank monthly.

63d vol-adj. mom. · 21d re-rank · Sharpe $1.756$ · ret. $35.1\%$ · turnover $22\times$

Now there are enough good options to be picky twice. The extra ranking earns its trading cost — which would have been wasted in a small kitchen.

03
Starting point

One knob: how cautious do you want to be?

Markowitz turns "what should I buy?" into a single dial. Crank it down and you chase return; crank it up and you chase calm. The slider on the right is that dial.

Formally: pick weights $w$ on the simplex to balance expected return $\hat\mu^\top w$ against risk $w^\top\hat\Sigma w$, scaled by a caution parameter $\lambda$.

Markowitz · 1952 $$\begin{aligned} \min_{w \in \Delta_n} \quad & \lambda\, w^\top \hat\Sigma w \;-\; \hat\mu^\top w \\ \text{s.t.} \quad & \mathbf{1}^\top w = 1, \quad w \ge 0. \end{aligned}$$

The optimum lives on the efficient frontier: the best return at each level of risk. The first-order condition

$$2\lambda\, \hat\Sigma\, w^\star \;-\; \hat\mu \;+\; \eta\, \mathbf{1} = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; w^\star(\lambda) \propto \hat\Sigma^{-1}(\hat\mu - \eta\mathbf{1})$$

is the formal version of the dial: bigger $\lambda$ pulls $w^\star$ toward the calmest portfolio; smaller $\lambda$ pushes it toward the highest-return one.

Why one dial isn't enough

The equation assumes you've already decided what can go in the basket. In a real system, that menu is also a choice — and so is how often you rebuild it. Those decisions sit upstream of $w$ and can dominate it.

04
From a static problem to a controller

Trading is fuel. Movement costs more than you'd think.

Each rebalance is a tug-of-war: the new "best" target pulls the portfolio one way, last week's holdings pull the other. The bigger the cost knob, the harder it is to leave home.

Let $w_{t^-}$ be where you are and $w_t$ where you'd like to be. The trading-cost term $\kappa\,\|w_t-w_{t^-}\|_1$ literally measures how far you'd have to move:

$$\max_{w_t \in \Delta} \;\; \alpha_t^\top w_t \;-\; \lambda\, w_t^\top \hat\Sigma_t w_t \;-\; \underbrace{\kappa\, \|w_t - w_{t^-}\|_1}_{\text{cost of moving}}.$$

Your current holdings are now part of the "state". Done across many periods, this becomes a planning problem — at each step, balance today's reward against tomorrow's value:

Bellman $$V_t(x_t) = \max_{a_t \in \mathcal{A}(x_t)} \Big\{\, g(x_t,a_t) + \delta\,\mathbb{E}\big[V_{t+1}(x_{t+1}) \mid x_t,a_t\big] \,\Big\}$$

with $g(x_t,a_t) = \mathbb{E}[w_t^\top r_{t+1}\mid \mathcal{F}_t] - \kappa\|w_t-w_{t^-}\|_1 - \rho\,\mathcal{R}(w_t,\mathcal{F}_t)$.

We don't actually solve Bellman. We approximate the optimal policy with a small set of simple, named recipes (equal weight, rank-and-hold) and pick among them. The point is just to see where the trading-cost tug binds.

05
The decomposition

Two stacked choices: what to consider, then how much of each.

Step 1, screen: pick which $K$ items from a long list. (The shopping list.) Step 2, weighting: pick how much of each. (The recipe.) Both are optimization choices; classical theory only handles step 2.

Index the choices by universe breadth $b$, screen rule $j$, and weighting controller $k$. The whole stack is one optimization:

$$\begin{aligned} \max_{b,\,j,\,k} \quad & \underbrace{\tfrac{\widehat{\mu}_{b,j,k}}{\widehat{\sigma}_{b,j,k}}}_{\text{risk-adj. return}} \;-\; \phi\,\underbrace{\widehat{\text{DD}}_{b,j,k}}_{\text{drawdown}} \;-\; \psi\,\underbrace{\widehat{\text{TO}}_{b,j,k}}_{\text{turnover}} \\[2pt] \text{s.t.} \quad & H_{b,j}(t) = S_j\!\left(\mathcal{U}_b(t),\mathcal{F}_t\right) && \text{(shopping list)} \\ & w_t = C_k\!\left(H_{b,j}(t),\mathcal{F}_t,w_{t^-}\right) && \text{(recipe)} \\ & |H_{b,j}(t)| \le K_{\max}, \quad w_t \in \Delta_{|H_{b,j}(t)|}. \end{aligned}$$

Step 1 · The shopping list

Score each candidate by some rule $q_i(t)$ and keep the top $K$. Formally a 0–1 cardinality problem:

$$\max_{z\in\{0,1\}^{|\mathcal{U}|}} \sum_{i\in\mathcal{U}} q_i(t)\, z_i \;\;\text{s.t.}\;\; \sum_i z_i \le K.$$

The score is the personality of the screen. We tested five:

$$\begin{aligned} q_i^{\text{mom},\tau} &= \tfrac{P_i(t)}{P_i(t-\tau)} - 1 && \text{recent winners} \\ q_i^{\text{vam},\tau} &= \tfrac{P_i(t)/P_i(t-\tau)\,-\,1}{\hat\sigma_{i,\tau}(t)+\varepsilon} && \text{winners, noise-discounted} \\ q_i^{\text{lvol},\tau} &= -\hat\sigma_{i,\tau}(t) && \text{calmest names} \\ q_i^{\text{cluster}} &= q_i^{\text{mom},63} && \text{winners, sector-capped} \end{aligned}$$

Step 2 · The recipe

Given a shopping list $H_t$, pick weights. Two recipes were tested.

Equal weight. Split evenly. No estimation, no opinions:

$$w_{i,t}^{\text{EW}} = \tfrac{1}{|H_t|}\,\mathbf{1}\{i \in H_t\}.$$

Rank-and-hold. Inside the list, keep only the top $k$ recent winners and weight them equally:

$$w_{i,t}^{\text{MRH},\tau,k} = \tfrac{1}{k}\,\mathbf{1}\!\left\{ i \in \operatorname{TopK}_{\ell\in H_t}\!\left(\tfrac{P_\ell(t)}{P_\ell(t-\tau)} - 1\right)\right\}.$$

It's a simple recipe, but it isn't free — every re-rank means trading. Whether the extra ranking is worth the trades is what slide 9 measures.

What we actually solve

The headline equation is mixed-integer and we don't solve it directly. We sample it: $3\!\times\!5\!\times\!4 = 60$ named combinations evaluated honestly, then we map the surface.
06
The shopping list is a decision

Different scoring rules pick different shopping lists.

Pick the top $K$ of 500 candidates

$K =$ 20
Try it. Toggle the score; the highlighted dots are the chosen list. Same 500 candidates, different rule — totally different list. Each rule selects a different region of the (volatility, momentum) plane.
The shopping list isn't cleaning — it's part of the recipe. Each scoring rule cooks a different meal even before any weights are chosen.

Formally, a screen $S_j$ is just a map: candidates in, list out, with a size cap.

$$S_j : \big(\mathcal{U}_b(t), \mathcal{F}_t\big) \longmapsto H_{b,j}(t), \qquad |H_{b,j}(t)| \le K.$$

What you'll see on slide 9 is that the right scoring rule depends on how big the kitchen is: recent winners win at $b{=}100$, calmest wins at $b{=}250$, noise-discounted winners win at $b{=}500$.

Common mistake: treating the shopping list as "preprocessing". In a stacked system it has at least as much leverage as the recipe — sometimes more.
07
How we kept ourselves honest

No peeking at tomorrow.

A backtest only counts if the strategy could have actually been run. So we fit each recipe using only data up to a date, then judge it on the next 90 days — and repeat.

Concretely: every screen score and every controller statistic is computed using prices through the training end $t^{\text{tr}}_s$ only. Net reward is then realized over the next window $[t^{\text{ev,start}}_s, t^{\text{ev,end}}_s]$, with trading costs subtracted:

$$R_s(b,j,k) \;=\; \underbrace{w_t^\top r_{t+1}}_{\text{gross return}} \;-\; \underbrace{\kappa\,\|w_t - w_{t^-}\|_1}_{\text{trading cost}}, \qquad t \in s.$$

The score for a whole stack $(b,j,k)$ is its average risk-adjusted return across folds:

$$\widehat{\text{SR}}(b,j,k) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{W}|} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{W}} \frac{\bar{R}_s(b,j,k)}{\hat\sigma_s(b,j,k)}.$$

Layer attribution

Which layer is actually pulling its weight?

To isolate the shopping list, average across all four recipes. To isolate the recipe, average across all five lists. That removes the other layer from the picture so you can see what each one contributes alone.

The operational rule of thumb

More optimization isn't always better. The extra ranking pays only when the kitchen is big enough that the new picks beat the trading bill: roughly, marginal gross alpha $> \kappa\,\|\Delta w\|_1$.

Diagnostics · being honest about luck

The exact winner is shaky. The pattern isn't.

We tested $20$ recipes per kitchen and reported the best one. If you try enough things, one of them will look great by accident. So we shake the data and ask: how often does the same recipe still come out on top?

Formally: $\widehat{a} = \arg\max_a \widehat{\text{SR}}(a)$ over $|\mathcal{A}| = 20$ candidates. Block-bootstrap the folds, recompute the best arm thousands of times, look at the distribution.

What this means honestly

Two of three CIs include zero, and the point-estimate winner repeats only $\sim$one-third of the time. Therefore the contribution is the layer-shift mechanism, not a deployable rule that "Top-500 + vol-adj. momentum + 21d rank-and-hold" is uniquely best.
A correct next step is to increase independent fold count via point-in-time universe membership, not to claim significance from the current sample.
12
Robustness · charging the optimizer for movement

Does charging more for trades change the answer?

Winner Sharpe across three cost levels

Read. The Top-100 and Top-250 winners hardly trade — flat lines. The Top-500 winner trades a lot ($22\times$ a year) and pays for it, but still keeps the lead even at $25$ bps.
If the extra ranking layer were just luck, a higher trading bill would expose it. It doesn't — the bill scales with turnover, but the winner still wins.

For the Top-500 winner:

$$\widehat{\text{SR}}_{500}^\star(0) = 1.81, \quad \widehat{\text{SR}}_{500}^\star(10) = 1.76, \quad \widehat{\text{SR}}_{500}^\star(25) = 1.67.$$

That's $\approx -0.005$ Sharpe per basis point of cost — a real bill, but not a fatal one. The same winning family is picked at every cost level in every kitchen, so the layer-shift story isn't an artifact of the cost we chose.

Where this is too gentle

Real trading bills aren't proportional. They get worse the more you push at once. The next study replaces $\kappa\|\Delta w\|_1$ with $\sum_i c_i|\Delta w_i| + m_i (\Delta w_i)^2$ — a quadratic punishment for big trades.
13
A head chef that learns the menu

Can a learned router beat "do whatever worked recently"?

We don't ask a neural net to design portfolios from scratch — that would be a black box. Instead we ask it to pick from the recipes we already understand. The question becomes: can a learner choose better than a simple "do whatever worked recently" rule?

Each recipe is an "arm" $a$. The router observes the state $s_t$ (time, kitchen size, trailing rewards) and outputs a probability over arms via softmax:

$$\pi_\theta(a \mid s_t) \;=\; \frac{\exp\bigl(f_\theta(s_t)_a\bigr)}{\sum_{a' \in \mathcal{A}} \exp\bigl(f_\theta(s_t)_{a'}\bigr)}.$$

It earns the realized net return, minus a small penalty for jumping recipe:

$$r^{\text{router}}_t \;=\; R_t(a_t) \;-\; \xi\,\mathbf{1}\{a_t \ne a_{t-1}\}.$$

Train it with the standard REINFORCE update — pull up the log-probability of arms that did better than expected:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_t (G_t - b_t)\,\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\right].$$

The fair comparison

The same reward stream is fed to four head chefs:

  • Learned (RL): the policy above.
  • Train-fixed: pick the recipe that did best in training, never change.
  • Trailing window: "do whatever worked the last few months." A dumb-but-honest online rule.
  • Random / Oracle: floor and ceiling.

What this tells us

The learner does better than holding one recipe, but it does worse than the dumb-but-honest "what worked recently" rule. That's a meaningful negative result — it says: in this finite sample, a recency rule already squeezes most of the predictive juice out of recent rewards. A learned router only earns its keep if it adds something more than recency, like context about the kitchen itself. That's the next study.

14
Where the evidence runs out

What this study does not yet establish.

A claim is only as strong as its limits. Each box below is a thing the current data cannot tell you — and a precise next experiment.

1 · Survivors only

The candidate kitchens $\mathcal{U}_b(t)$ are today's liquid names, not the names that were investable on each date. A company that went bust in 2020 isn't in the data. Fix: rebuild point-in-time membership.

2 · Few honest folds

The big kitchen has only $9$ independent windows. The router has even fewer. With a small sample, two of three winner gaps stretch through zero — the rankings could flip on noise.

3 · Picked from many tries

Each kitchen reports the best of $20$ recipes. The best of many tries is bigger than the typical try. Deflated-Sharpe-style corrections aren't yet baked into the headline numbers.

4 · Trading bills are too gentle

Current cost is a flat $\kappa\|\Delta w\|_1$. Real bills get worse the more you push at once: $c_i|\Delta w_i| + m_i(\Delta w_i)^2$. Adding non-linear impact would punish the active recipe more.

5 · The router can't see the kitchen

The learner only sees recent rewards and the kitchen's name — not its shape (pairwise correlation, PC1 share, dispersion, vol-of-vol). With no kitchen context, beating "what worked recently" is genuinely hard.

6 · We sampled, didn't solve

The headline equation on slide 5 is mixed-integer. We tried 60 named recipes; we didn't solve the integer program. Whether a continuous relaxation (e.g. $\ell_1$-regularized) recovers the same layer-shift is open.

Each box above is the path from "there's a mechanism here" to "here's a rule you can actually run."

15
How this connects

Standing on which shoulders.

Every piece of the stack borrows from a known idea. The contribution isn't any single piece — it's joining them into one map and benchmarking honestly.

The risk–return dial

Markowitz (1952). Slide 3 is just the textbook frontier — risk vs. return as one knob.

"Trading is fuel"

Gârleanu & Pedersen (2013) show that the optimal dynamic portfolio only partially adjusts toward its target — the rest is held back by friction. Slide 4 is the discrete sibling of that idea.

"You can't peek at tomorrow"

Cover (1991) and the online-portfolio literature insist policies are judged sequentially, on data the system actually saw. The trailing-window router is exactly that idea.

Learners over recipes

Jiang & Liang (2017), Zhang et al. (2020) apply policy gradients to allocation. The rule we borrow is strict: a learner is only interesting if it beats transparent online rules on the same reward stream.

What's new here

One layered map — universe, list, recipe, friction, head chef — plus an honest evaluation surface that shows which layer is binding under which kitchen. And a learned head chef that doesn't yet beat "what worked recently" — which is itself the result.

16
Conclusion

Three things to remember.

  1. Allocation is a stack, not a single problem. Where you shop, what you list, how much of each, how often you redo it — all are optimization choices. Textbook theory only handles one of them.
  2. The binding layer changes with the kitchen. Small kitchen: picking does the work. Medium: filtering for calm wins. Big: a second pickier layer earns its trading bill. Formally, the binding layer shifts with universe breadth $b$.
  3. Fancy must beat dumb-but-honest. A learned head chef that can't beat "do whatever worked recently" isn't ready to deploy — no matter how clever the architecture.

The question shifts from "which portfolio rule wins?" to "which layer is binding in this kitchen, right now?"
